Delhi's Winter Smog Crisis:
Chemical Processes, Health Impacts, and Emission Controls

By G.V. Kumar • Retd. Meteorologist, IMD, RMC Chennai • 5 February 2026

Introduction

Every winter, Delhi transforms into one of the world's most polluted cities. As temperatures drop and winds calm, a toxic blanket of smog envelops the metropolitan region, affecting over 30 million residents. The Air Quality Index (AQI) regularly exceeds 400—classified as ‘severe’ or ‘hazardous’—with PM (particulate matter) 2.5 concentrations reaching 15–20 times the World Health Organization's safe limits. This annual crisis is not merely an environmental problem but a severe public health emergency that costs thousands of lives and affects the development of millions of children.

Understanding the chemistry behind smog formation, its dispersion mechanisms, and its devastating health impacts is crucial for developing effective solutions. This article explores the complex interplay of atmospheric chemistry, meteorology, and human activities that create Delhi's winter smog, and examines both the immediate and long-term consequences for public health.

The Science of Smog Formation

Understanding Winter Smog

Delhi's winter smog is a complex mixture of particulate matter (PM2.5 and PM10), sulfur dioxide (SO₂), nitrogen oxides (NOₓ), carbon monoxide (CO), ozone (O₃), and volatile organic compounds (VOCs). Unlike the photochemical smog common in summer months, winter smog is dominated by primary pollutants that accumulate due to specific meteorological conditions and intensive heating activities.

The Role of Temperature Inversion

The critical factor in Delhi's winter air quality crisis is temperature inversion. Normally, air temperature decreases with altitude, allowing warm, polluted air to rise and disperse. However, during winter nights, the ground cools rapidly, creating a layer of cold, dense air near the surface trapped beneath warmer air above. This atmospheric lid prevents vertical mixing, trapping pollutants in the breathing zone for days or even weeks. Combined with low wind speeds typical of North Indian winters, pollutants accumulate to dangerous concentrations.

Chemical Processes in Smog Formation and Dispersion

Sulfur Dioxide Transformation

Sulfur dioxide, primarily from coal combustion in power plants, industries, and diesel vehicles, undergoes several transformation pathways that convert gaseous pollutants into particulate matter:

Aqueous Phase Oxidation

In the presence of fog droplets, SO₂ dissolves and oxidizes through the following reactions:

SO₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₃
H₂SO₃ + ½O₂ → H₂SO₄

The resulting sulfuric acid either remains in fog droplets or forms fine sulfate particles when moisture evaporates. This process is accelerated by metal catalysts (iron, manganese) present on particulate matter, making industrial emissions particularly harmful.

Gas-Phase Oxidation

During daylight hours, hydroxyl radicals (OH•)—the atmosphere's primary cleaning agent—attack SO₂:

SO₂ + OH• → HOSO₂•
HOSO₂• + O₂ → SO₃ + HO₂•
SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄

However, winter's weak sunlight and short days severely limit hydroxyl radical formation, significantly slowing this cleansing mechanism when Delhi needs it most.

Nitrogen Oxides Chemistry

Nitrogen oxides (NOₓ), primarily nitric oxide (NO) and nitrogen dioxide (NO₂) from vehicle emissions and industrial processes, play dual roles in atmospheric chemistry:

Ozone Formation and Destruction

During daytime, NO₂ photolysis creates ozone:

NO₂ + sunlight → NO + O
O + O₂ → O₃

However, at night, fresh NO emissions from vehicles actually destroy ozone:

O₃ + NO → NO₂ + O₂

This explains why ozone levels can be relatively low during Delhi's winter smog episodes despite high NOₓ concentrations—the abundant NO from continuous traffic depletes ozone faster than weak winter sunlight can regenerate it.

Nitric Acid and Nitrate Formation

NO₂ also converts to nitric acid through hydroxyl radical reactions:

NO₂ + OH• → HNO₃

The nitric acid reacts with atmospheric ammonia (from agricultural activities and waste) to form ammonium nitrate particles:

HNO₃ + NH₃ → NH₄NO₃

These secondary particles significantly contribute to PM2.5 concentrations, especially in humid conditions where they absorb water and grow larger, reducing visibility and increasing health risks.

Carbon Dioxide and Particulate Matter

While carbon dioxide (CO₂) is chemically stable in the lower atmosphere and doesn't undergo significant transformation, carbon monoxide (CO) from incomplete combustion is a major concern. CO lacks the reactivity needed to chemically disperse but poses immediate health threats by binding to hemoglobin more effectively than oxygen, reducing blood oxygen-carrying capacity.

Primary particulate matter from combustion sources combines with secondary particles formed through the chemical reactions described above. In Delhi's winter fog, these particles absorb water and grow through hygroscopic growth, creating the characteristic thick haze that reduces visibility to mere meters and concentrates toxic substances in the breathing zone.

Why Chemical Dispersion Fails in Winter

The atmospheric chemistry processes described above theoretically cleanse polluted air. However, Delhi's winter conditions create a perfect storm that overwhelms these natural mechanisms:

  • Limited hydroxyl radical formation due to weak sunlight drastically slows gas-phase oxidation reactions
  • Temperature inversions trap pollutants near ground level, preventing vertical dispersion. In winter nights and mornings in Delhi, a layer of warmer air sits above colder surface air and acts like a lid, so polluted air cannot rise and disperse
  • Weak winds and low mixing height over the Indo-Gangetic plains mean pollutants from Delhi, Haryana, and upwind states stay concentrated close to the ground instead of being diluted
  • High humidity and dense fog droplets form more easily in winter, growing around pollution particles (aerosols) to create thick persistent fog
  • Low wind speeds reduce horizontal dilution
  • Continuous emissions far exceed the atmosphere's limited cleansing capacity

The result is pollutant accumulation that can only be broken by strong meteorological events (high winds, rain) or significant emission reductions—chemical transformation alone cannot keep pace.

Devastating Health Impacts

Scale of the Health Crisis

Delhi's winter air quality represents one of the most severe urban health crises globally. Studies estimate 10,000–30,000 premature deaths annually in Delhi attributable to air pollution, with life expectancy reduced by approximately 10–12 years for long-term residents. During severe pollution episodes, daily mortality increases by 2–5%, overwhelming healthcare facilities.

Respiratory System Damage — Immediate Effects

Fine particulate matter (PM2.5) and toxic gases cause immediate respiratory distress. PM2.5 (fine particles) measures 2.5 micrometers or less in diameter and can cause respiratory, cardiovascular and lung issues. PM10 (coarse particles) up to 10 micrometers in diameter can enter the nose and throat. Emergency room visits for asthma attacks increase dramatically during smog episodes, with even well-controlled patients experiencing severe exacerbations. The airways become inflamed, producing excess mucus and triggering coughing, wheezing, and chest tightness.

Long-term Consequences

Chronic exposure leads to irreversible lung damage. Children growing up in Delhi show permanently reduced lung capacity compared to those in less polluted areas—a deficit that persists throughout life. Adults experience accelerated lung function decline, with many developing chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) years earlier than expected. The International Agency for Research on Cancer has classified outdoor air pollution as a Group 1 carcinogen.

Cardiovascular System Damage

The cardiovascular impacts of air pollution are often underestimated but equally severe. Ultrafine particles (PM2.5 and smaller) penetrate deep into lungs and cross into the bloodstream, triggering systemic inflammation and oxidative stress throughout the cardiovascular system.

Studies demonstrate that for every 10 μg/m³ increase in PM2.5 concentration, heart attack risk increases by 1–3%. During severe smog episodes when PM2.5 exceeds 300 μg/m³, Delhi hospitals report surges in heart attacks and strokes.

Neurological and Cognitive Impacts

Emerging research reveals alarming effects on brain health. Ultrafine particles can reach the brain through multiple pathways—crossing the blood-brain barrier, traveling along olfactory nerves from the nose, or entering through compromised blood vessels. Once in the brain, they trigger neuroinflammation and oxidative damage.

Adults exposed to high pollution levels show accelerated cognitive decline, with increased risks of dementia and Alzheimer's disease. Children face particularly severe neurodevelopmental risks, with research showing associations between pollution exposure and reduced academic performance, attention deficits, and behavioral problems.

Vulnerable Populations

Children are disproportionately affected—they breathe more rapidly than adults relative to body weight, and their developing organs are more vulnerable to toxic insults. Playground hours expose them during high pollution periods.

Elderly residents face the highest mortality risk during pollution episodes. Their reduced physiological reserves and pre-existing conditions make them extremely vulnerable.

Pregnant women face additional risks including increased rates of low birth weight babies, preterm births, and potential impacts on fetal brain development.

Emission Control Measures at Source

Given that chemical atmospheric processes cannot adequately cleanse Delhi's air under winter conditions, controlling emissions at their source is imperative. Delhi's GRAP (Graded Response Action Plan) Stage 4 (AQI >450) was enforced on 13 December 2025, banning construction activities, checking polluting industries, banning older diesel vehicles, deploying anti-smog guns, and resorting to hybrid schooling.

Transportation Sector Controls

India has implemented Bharat Stage VI (BS-VI) norms equivalent to European Euro 6 standards, mandating three-way catalytic converters, diesel particulate filters (trapping over 95% of particulate emissions), and selective catalytic reduction systems (reducing nitrogen oxide emissions by up to 90%).

Delhi has implemented aggressive policies to phase out old polluting vehicles, promote electric vehicles, expand the metro network, and implement odd-even vehicle rationing during severe pollution episodes.

Industrial Emission Controls

Industries must install pollution control equipment: electrostatic precipitators (capturing 90–99% of particles), bag filters, wet scrubbers, and flue gas desulfurization systems:

SO₂ + CaCO₃ + ½O₂ → CaSO₄ + CO₂

Agricultural Practices

Crop residue burning in neighboring states like Haryana and Punjab contributes significantly to Delhi's pollution during October–November. Solutions include providing farmers with machinery (Happy Seeders, balers, rakes) and promoting use of crop residue for biogas, cattle feed, composting, or manufacturing.

Construction and Dust Management

Mandatory measures include water spraying on exposed soil, covering construction materials, wheel washing facilities, and mechanized sweeping. At the indoor level, portable HEPA-based air cleaners reduce what people actually inhale. Cloud seeding trials in 2025 yielded limited success.

Groundwater Depletion — An Under-Recognized Factor

Delhi's smog is not only a pollution problem—it is a collision of atmospheric physics, urban development, agricultural practices and climate patterns. Groundwater depletion represents an under-recognized hydrological factor that indirectly amplifies pollution severity. In 2023, nearly 99% of groundwater was extracted while recharge declined due to reduced rainfall in winter. As water tables decline, dry soil contributes to airborne dust (PM10 and PM2.5), and reduced vegetation weakens natural filtering of airborne particulates.

Regulatory Framework

The Graded Response Action Plan (GRAP) implements escalating restrictions based on pollution levels. Real-time air quality monitoring networks enable tracking, but penalties for violations are often insufficient to deter non-compliance. Economic instruments including environmental taxes and emission trading schemes can incentivize emission reductions.

Conclusion: A Crisis Demanding Urgent Action

Delhi's winter smog represents a perfect storm of atmospheric chemistry, meteorological conditions, and anthropogenic emissions. The chemical processes that naturally cleanse the atmosphere are overwhelmed by the sheer volume of emissions and suppressed by winter's meteorological conditions.

The health consequences are catastrophic and multi-generational. Tens of thousands of premature deaths annually, millions suffering from respiratory and cardiovascular diseases, children growing up with impaired lung and brain development—these are not abstract statistics but a humanitarian crisis unfolding in real-time.

The solution lies not in waiting for atmospheric chemistry to disperse pollutants but in aggressive, sustained emission reductions at source. This requires simultaneous action across all sectors: transportation, industry, power generation, agriculture, construction, and waste management.

The chemistry is clear, the health impacts are documented, and the solutions are known. What remains is the challenge of implementation—marshalling the resources, coordination, and sustained commitment necessary to give Delhi's residents, particularly its children, the fundamental right to breathe clean air.
