Urban Heat Island Effect in Indian Cities:
A Chronological Scientific Review

By G.V. Kumar • Retd. Meteorologist, IMD, RMC Chennai • 21 January 2025

Abstract

This research paper provides a comprehensive chronological analysis of the Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect in Indian cities based on multiple studies and documents from 2010 to 2024. The paper integrates land‐use change assessments, temperature variation studies, remote‐sensing analyses, and recent climate observations to map the progression and intensification of UHI impacts. It highlights environmental, health, and urban‐planning implications while identifying mitigation strategies informed by both scientific research and traditional architectural practices.

1. Introduction

The Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect refers to the increased temperature in urban areas compared to their rural surroundings, primarily due to anthropogenic activities, land‐use change, and infrastructural development. Indian metropolitan regions such as Bengaluru, Chennai, and Mysuru have experienced progressive intensification of UHI over the past decades. This paper synthesizes information from early foundational studies around 2010, mid‐term analyses, and recent high‐resolution climate observations from 2021–2024.

2. Early Evidence of UHI in Indian Cities (2010 Era)

Early research around 2010 focused on quantifying temperature variations and mapping land‐use/land‐cover (LULC) changes in Bengaluru. Studies revealed that urban cores were 1.5°C to 4°C warmer than surrounding rural zones, with the greatest differences observed at night. Remote sensing tools such as Landsat TM/ETM+ imagery were widely used to derive Land Surface Temperature (LST) and vegetation indices (NDVI). A notable finding was the inverse relationship between vegetation density and surface temperature. Urban expansion between 1973 and 2010 resulted in substantial loss of green cover, contributing to worsening UHI intensity.

3. Continued Urbanization and Land Surface Transformation (2010–2020)

Research conducted during the 2010–2020 period continued to demonstrate strong correlations between urbanization, loss of natural ecosystems, and rising ambient and surface temperatures. Bengaluru experienced rapid infrastructure growth and significant encroachment of wetlands and lakes. Studies emphasized the role of vegetated areas such as Lalbagh, Cubbon Park, IISc campus, and Mysore Zoo as natural cooling zones with temperature reductions between 2°C and 4°C compared to built‐up surroundings. These findings reinforced calls for integrating urban forestry, conservation planning, and climate‐responsive architecture into city development.

4. Recent Climatic Trends and UHI Intensification (2021–2024)

Recent studies from 2021 to 2024 highlight unprecedented changes in climatic patterns. In April 2024, Bengaluru recorded 37.6°C—its second‐highest temperature—and unusually warm nights of 24.5°C. The city also experienced its wettest year in 2022 with 169 cm of rainfall. A 2022 land‐use study reported a 51.8% increase in built‐up area between 1973 and 2022, accompanied by a 26.2% decline in green spaces and water bodies. These changes intensify UHI conditions, disrupt local microclimates, heighten heat‐related health risks, increase energy consumption, and contribute to biodiversity loss. Key vegetated zones such as IISc campus, GKVK, and Mysore Zoo continue to function as temperature‐moderating buffers.

5. Methodology

This review synthesizes insights from satellite‐based remote sensing analyses, land‐use change mapping, ground‐based climate observations, and scientific publications. Key methodologies referenced across studies include NDVI computation, LST extraction, GIS‐based spatiotemporal analysis, and microclimate monitoring within urban green spaces.

6. Discussion

The chronological progression of research reveals an increasingly strong link between rapid urban expansion and UHI severity. Modern building materials, heat‐retentive surfaces, and compact architectural forms exacerbate heat accumulation. Conversely, areas with high vegetation density demonstrate significant cooling effects. Traditional Indian architectural practices, such as terracotta roofs, exposed brick walls, verandahs, and courtyard‐based layouts, offer valuable guidance for climate‐adaptive urban design.

7. Conclusion

The UHI effect in Indian cities has intensified significantly over the past decade due to changes in land use, reduced vegetation, and the proliferation of heat‐absorbing infrastructure. As climate extremes become more frequent, there is an urgent need for nature‐based solutions, sustainable building design, policy interventions, and citizen‐based climate monitoring. Integrating UHI considerations into city master plans is essential for ensuring long‐term environmental resilience and public health protection.
