Urban Heat Island Effect and NDVI Analysis in Indian Cities:
A Comprehensive Scientific Review

By G.V. Kumar • Retd. Meteorologist, IMD, RMC Chennai • 12 February 2026

Abstract

This comprehensive research paper provides an integrated analysis of the Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect in Indian cities, combining chronological climate observations with remote sensing methodologies, particularly Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) analysis. Drawing from multiple studies conducted between 2010 and 2024, this review synthesizes land‐use change assessments, temperature variation studies, satellite‐based vegetation monitoring, and recent climate observations to map the progression and intensification of UHI impacts across major Indian metropolitan regions. The paper highlights environmental, health, and urban‐planning implications while identifying mitigation strategies informed by both modern scientific research and traditional architectural practices.

1. Introduction

The Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect represents one of the most significant climatic impacts of urbanization, characterized by elevated temperatures in urban areas compared to their rural surroundings. This phenomenon results from a complex interplay of anthropogenic activities, land‐use modifications, and infrastructural development that fundamentally alter the thermal properties of urban landscapes.

Indian metropolitan regions, particularly Bengaluru, Chennai, and Mysuru, have experienced progressive intensification of UHI effects over recent decades, driven by rapid urbanization, population growth, and expanding built environments. The relationship between urban vegetation and surface temperature has emerged as a critical factor in understanding and mitigating UHI intensity, with the Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) serving as a key remote sensing tool for quantifying this relationship.

This paper synthesizes findings from early foundational studies around 2010, mid‐term analyses, and recent high‐resolution climate observations from 2021–2024, integrating remote sensing data, particularly NDVI measurements, with ground‐based climate monitoring to provide a comprehensive understanding of UHI dynamics in Indian cities.

2. NDVI Methodology and Remote Sensing Approach

2.1 Understanding NDVI

The Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) is a standardized remote sensing metric that quantifies vegetation health and density by measuring the difference between near‐infrared (NIR) and visible red light reflected by vegetation. The NDVI formula is expressed as:

NDVI = (NIR − Red) / (NIR + Red)

NDVI values range from −1 to +1, where higher values (0.6 to 0.9) indicate dense, healthy vegetation, moderate values (0.2 to 0.5) suggest sparse or stressed vegetation, and low or negative values indicate bare soil, water, or built‐up areas. This index exploits the unique spectral signature of healthy vegetation, which strongly absorbs red light for photosynthesis while reflecting near‐infrared radiation.

2.2 Satellite Platforms and Data Sources

Multiple satellite systems have been employed for NDVI and Land Surface Temperature (LST) analysis in Indian urban studies:

  • Landsat Program (TM, ETM+, OLI sensors): Provides consistent 30‐meter resolution multispectral imagery with thermal bands for LST retrieval, forming the backbone of long‐term urban monitoring studies
  • Sentinel‐2: Offers enhanced spatial resolution (10–20 meters) and improved temporal frequency for contemporary vegetation monitoring
  • MODIS (Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer): Enables daily observations at coarser resolution, suitable for large‐scale regional assessments
  • Indian Remote Sensing Satellites (IRS series): Provides complementary data with varied spatial and spectral resolutions

2.3 Integration with Land Surface Temperature

The relationship between NDVI and Land Surface Temperature forms a cornerstone of UHI research. Studies consistently demonstrate a strong inverse correlation: areas with high NDVI (dense vegetation) exhibit significantly lower surface temperatures, while areas with low NDVI (urban built‐up regions) show elevated temperatures. This relationship is quantified through regression analysis and spatiotemporal correlation studies, revealing that vegetated areas can be 2–4°C cooler than adjacent built‐up zones.

3. Early Evidence of UHI in Indian Cities (2010 Era)

Foundational research conducted around 2010 established the baseline understanding of UHI dynamics in Indian cities, with Bengaluru emerging as a primary case study. These early investigations employed Landsat TM/ETM+ imagery to derive both Land Surface Temperature (LST) and vegetation indices, revealing critical patterns in the urban thermal landscape.

Key findings from this period demonstrated that urban cores were consistently 1.5°C to 4°C warmer than surrounding rural zones, with the greatest temperature differentials observed during nighttime hours. This nocturnal intensification reflects the thermal mass properties of urban infrastructure, which absorbs solar radiation during the day and releases it slowly at night, preventing urban areas from cooling as rapidly as vegetated or rural landscapes. The inverse relationship between NDVI and surface temperature was clearly established, with correlation coefficients typically exceeding −0.7, indicating that areas with higher vegetation density consistently exhibited lower surface temperatures. Analysis of historical satellite imagery documented substantial urban expansion between 1973 and 2010, characterized by significant loss of green cover and a corresponding increase in built‐up areas, directly contributing to worsening UHI intensity.

4. Continued Urbanization and Land Surface Transformation (2010–2020)

The decade spanning 2010 to 2020 witnessed accelerated urban development across Indian cities, accompanied by increasingly sophisticated remote sensing analyses. Research during this period continued to demonstrate robust correlations between urbanization patterns, ecosystem degradation, and rising temperatures at both ambient and surface levels.

Bengaluru experienced particularly rapid infrastructural growth during this period, characterized by significant encroachment on wetlands, lakes, and other natural ecosystems. NDVI‐based analyses revealed progressive fragmentation and reduction of vegetated patches, with remaining green spaces becoming increasingly isolated within the urban matrix. Despite this overall trend of vegetation loss, specific vegetated areas maintained their role as critical cooling zones within the urban environment.

Studies identified several key green spaces that demonstrated measurable cooling effects: Lalbagh Botanical Garden, Cubbon Park, the Indian Institute of Science (IISc) campus, and Mysore Zoo all exhibited surface temperatures 2°C to 4°C lower than surrounding built‐up areas. These findings reinforced the importance of preserving and expanding urban green spaces, leading to increased calls for integrating urban forestry, conservation planning, and climate‐responsive architecture into city development frameworks.

5. Recent Climatic Trends and UHI Intensification (2021–2024)

Recent studies from 2021 to 2024 document unprecedented changes in climatic patterns and accelerated UHI intensification across Indian metropolitan regions. These contemporary analyses benefit from improved satellite sensors, higher temporal resolution data, and more sophisticated analytical techniques.

In April 2024, Bengaluru recorded a temperature of 37.6°C, marking its second‐highest temperature on record, accompanied by unusually warm nights reaching 24.5°C. The city also experienced its wettest year on record in 2022, with total annual rainfall reaching 169 cm, indicating increasing climatic variability and extremes. A comprehensive 2022 land‐use study reported a 51.8% increase in built‐up area between 1973 and 2022, accompanied by a devastating 26.2% decline in green spaces and water bodies.

NDVI analysis during this period revealed not only continued decline in overall vegetation cover but also degradation in the health and density of remaining vegetated areas. Time‐series NDVI data showed declining trends even in protected green spaces, suggesting that remaining urban vegetation faces increasing stress from heat, water scarcity, and pollution.

These changes intensify UHI conditions through multiple mechanisms: reduced evapotranspiration from vegetation loss decreases natural cooling; increased impervious surfaces enhance heat absorption and storage; disrupted local microclimates alter wind patterns and humidity levels; and heightened heat‐related health risks affect vulnerable populations. Additional consequences include elevated energy consumption for cooling, contributing to greenhouse gas emissions, and biodiversity loss as urban habitats become increasingly inhospitable. Despite overall degradation, key vegetated zones such as IISc campus, Gandhi Krishi Vignana Kendra (GKVK), and Mysore Zoo continue to function as temperature‐moderating buffers, though their effectiveness may be diminishing.

6. Integrated Research Methodology

This comprehensive review synthesizes insights from multiple methodological approaches employed across studies spanning 2010 to 2024:

  • Satellite‐Based Remote Sensing: Multi‐temporal analysis using Landsat, Sentinel‐2, and MODIS imagery for NDVI computation and LST extraction across multiple time periods
  • Land‐Use/Land‐Cover (LULC) Change Mapping: Classification and change detection analysis to quantify urbanization patterns, vegetation loss, and built‐up expansion over five decades
  • GIS‐Based Spatiotemporal Analysis: Geographic Information Systems integration for spatial pattern analysis, hot‐spot identification, and trend mapping
  • Ground‐Based Climate Observations: Integration of meteorological station data from IMD and local urban climate monitoring networks for validation and complementary analysis
  • Microclimate Monitoring: Detailed measurements within urban green spaces to quantify localized cooling effects and establish vegetation‐temperature relationships

7. Discussion

7.1 The Vegetation‐Temperature Nexus

The chronological progression of research reveals an increasingly strong and well‐documented link between rapid urban expansion, vegetation loss, and UHI severity. The NDVI‐LST relationship demonstrates that vegetation plays a multifaceted role in urban cooling: evapotranspiration removes heat from the environment through latent heat flux; shading reduces direct solar radiation absorption by surfaces; increased surface albedo from vegetated areas reflects more solar radiation; and modified air circulation patterns enhance natural ventilation. These cooling mechanisms are most effective in areas with high vegetation density and connectivity, suggesting that not just the total amount but also the spatial configuration of urban green space matters significantly.

7.2 Urban Design and Material Considerations

Modern building materials and urban design approaches significantly contribute to heat accumulation. Concrete, asphalt, and metal surfaces exhibit high thermal mass and low albedo, absorbing substantial solar radiation during the day and releasing it slowly at night. The proliferation of compact architectural forms with limited natural ventilation, combined with widespread use of air conditioning that rejects heat to the outdoor environment, creates positive feedback loops that intensify UHI effects. In contrast, areas with high vegetation density demonstrate significant cooling effects, with surface temperatures 2–4°C lower than adjacent built‐up zones and potential air temperature reductions of 1–2°C extending beyond the vegetated area itself.

7.3 Lessons from Traditional Architecture

Traditional Indian architectural practices, developed over centuries in response to local climatic conditions, offer valuable guidance for climate‐adaptive urban design. Key features include: terracotta roof tiles that provide thermal mass with lower conductivity than modern materials; exposed brick walls with natural thermal regulation properties; verandahs and covered walkways that provide shading while maintaining air circulation; courtyard‐based layouts that create microclimates and enhance natural ventilation; orientation and building placement optimized for wind flow and solar shading; and integration of water features and vegetation within building complexes. These principles, informed by empirical observation and refined through generations, represent time‐tested strategies that can be adapted and integrated into contemporary urban planning and architectural practice.

8. Mitigation Strategies and Recommendations

Based on the integrated analysis of NDVI‐temperature relationships and historical urban development patterns, this review identifies several priority strategies for UHI mitigation:

  • Urban Green Infrastructure Expansion: Protect and expand existing green spaces; create new parks, gardens, and tree‐lined corridors; implement green roofs and vertical gardens on buildings; establish connected green networks rather than isolated patches; and prioritize native species that are well‐adapted to local conditions and require minimal irrigation
  • Sustainable Building Design: Incorporate natural ventilation and passive cooling strategies; use high‐albedo (reflective) materials for roofs and pavements; integrate traditional architectural elements adapted for modern structures; mandate green building standards in development regulations; and promote mixed‐use development to reduce transportation‐related heat generation
  • Water Body Restoration: Restore degraded lakes, wetlands, and urban watercourses; prevent further encroachment on existing water bodies; maintain adequate water levels through rainwater harvesting and treated wastewater reuse; and recognize the cooling effects of water features in urban design
  • Policy Interventions: Integrate UHI considerations into city master plans and development codes; establish mandatory NDVI thresholds or green space requirements for new developments; implement heat action plans for public health protection during extreme events; create incentive programs for building owners who implement cooling strategies; and require Environmental Impact Assessments to include UHI analysis
  • Monitoring and Research: Establish comprehensive urban climate monitoring networks; conduct regular NDVI and LST assessments using satellite data; develop city‐specific UHI models for scenario planning; engage citizens in temperature monitoring and data collection; and promote interdisciplinary research linking climate science, public health, urban planning, and social equity

9. Conclusion

The integration of chronological climate observations with NDVI‐based remote sensing analysis provides compelling evidence for the intensification of the Urban Heat Island effect in Indian cities over the past decades. The 51.8% increase in built‐up area and 26.2% decline in green spaces documented between 1973 and 2022 in Bengaluru exemplify a pattern repeated across Indian metropolitan regions, with profound implications for urban climate, public health, energy consumption, and environmental sustainability.

The strong inverse correlation between NDVI and land surface temperature, consistently demonstrated across multiple studies and time periods, underscores the critical importance of urban vegetation in moderating thermal extremes. As climate change intensifies background warming and extreme heat events become more frequent, the cooling services provided by urban green infrastructure will become increasingly vital for maintaining livable urban environments.

There is an urgent need for nature‐based solutions, sustainable building design, comprehensive policy interventions, and citizen‐based climate monitoring. Integrating UHI considerations into city master plans, informed by both modern scientific understanding and traditional architectural wisdom, is essential for ensuring long‐term environmental resilience, public health protection, and social equity in India’s rapidly growing cities.

Future research should focus on developing localized UHI models, assessing the effectiveness of different mitigation strategies in Indian urban contexts, understanding the social dimensions of heat vulnerability, and establishing long‐term monitoring programs that integrate ground‐based measurements with satellite observations. Only through sustained scientific inquiry, coupled with decisive policy action and community engagement, can Indian cities successfully navigate the challenges posed by intensifying urban heat islands in an era of climate change.
